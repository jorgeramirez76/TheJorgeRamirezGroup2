Home Styles & Housing Stock in Montclair

Montclair is famous for its architectural diversity and beautiful historic homes. As someone who's walked through hundreds of these properties over the years, I can tell you that Montclair's housing stock is truly special – from grand Victorian mansions to charming Craftsman bungalows, the architecture tells the story of a community that has valued beauty and craftsmanship for over 150 years.

Architectural Styles You'll Find

Victorian Homes (1870s – 1920s)

Montclair's signature style – beautiful Queen Anne, Colonial Revival, and Shingle-style homes from the late 1800s and early 1900s. These properties often feature wraparound porches, intricate woodwork, stained glass windows, and multiple fireplaces. Many sit on generous lots of 0.25 to 0.5 acres. The Ingleside section and parts of Upper Montclair have some of the finest examples.

Tudor Revival Homes (1910s – 1930s)

Distinctive half-timbered properties with steeply pitched gable roofs, arched doorways, and decorative stonework. These homes offer tremendous character and are particularly common in the Mountainside and Upper Montclair areas. Expect to find original details like leaded glass windows and wrought iron hardware.

Colonial Homes (1920s – 1970s)

Classic center-hall designs from various eras, ranging from modest three-bedroom homes to substantial five-bedroom center-hall colonials. These are the workhorse of Montclair's market – practical layouts that work well for modern families. Lot sizes vary from 0.15 to 0.4 acres depending on the neighborhood.

Craftsman Bungalows (1910s – 1930s)

Arts and Crafts-style homes with built-ins, exposed beams, and period details. These single-story or 1.5-story homes offer tremendous charm and are often more affordable entry points into desirable neighborhoods. The South End and parts of central Montclair have excellent examples.

Mid-Century Modern (1950s – 1970s)

Distinctive homes from the 1950s-60s with architectural significance – open floor plans, large windows, and integration with natural surroundings. These are less common but highly sought after by design-conscious buyers.

Condominiums and Townhomes

Options in downtown areas and converted historic buildings, ranging from studios starting around $300,000 to luxury two-bedroom units exceeding $700,000. The Seymour Street corridor and Bloomfield Avenue areas offer the most inventory.

Lot Sizes and Property Features

Montclair's lot sizes vary dramatically by neighborhood:

  • Upper Montclair: 0.33 to 1+ acre lots are common
  • Central Montclair: 0.20 to 0.35 acre lots typical
  • South End: 0.12 to 0.25 acre lots
  • Downtown Area: Smaller lots, some under 0.10 acres

Age of Housing Stock

Approximately 65% of Montclair's housing was built before 1960, with significant construction in the 1920s and 1950s. This historic character is part of what makes Montclair special, but it also means buyers should budget for updates and maintenance. Schools: Magnet Excellence

The Montclair Public School District uses a magnet school model that sets it apart from virtually every other district in New Jersey. Rather than attending their neighborhood school, families rank their preferences and students are assigned based on a lottery system that considers proximity and sibling priority. This creates incredibly diverse, vibrant school communities where children from all backgrounds learn together.

Elementary Magnet Schools (K-5)

  • Bradford School: Environmental science magnet with greenhouse and outdoor learning spaces. Strong focus on sustainability and nature-based education.
  • Charles H. Bullock School: STEM and sustainability magnet with advanced technology integration and project-based learning.
  • Edgemont Montessori School: Authentic Montessori education following Dr. Montessori's methods with multi-age classrooms and self-directed learning.
  • Hillside School: Gifted and talented magnet serving identified students with accelerated curriculum and enrichment opportunities.
  • Nishuane School: Arts and global studies magnet with strong music, visual arts, and cultural awareness programs.
  • Watchung School: Science and technology magnet with robust STEM programming and technology integration.

Middle School (6-8)

  • Glenfield Middle School: A rating; diverse programs and strong academics. Known for its inclusive environment and performing arts programs. Located in a beautiful historic building.
  • Mount Hebron Middle School: A rating; STEM and arts focus with award-winning robotics and music programs. Features modern facilities and strong community partnerships.

High School (9-12)

  • Montclair High School: A+ rating; one of NJ's most diverse and acclaimed high schools with approximately 2,000 students. Notable for exceptional performing arts (theater program rivals private schools), strong academics, and championship athletics. Offers over 20 AP courses and has a graduation rate exceeding 95%.

NYC Commute & Transportation

Montclair is one of New Jersey's most transit-friendly suburbs, served by six NJ Transit stations on the Montclair-Boonton Line. This gives residents unmatched flexibility – if one station is crowded or parking is full, you have five other options. As someone who helps buyers prioritize commute convenience, I always map out the closest stations and parking situations for my clients.

Train Stations

  • Upper Montclair: To NYC Penn Station – 40-50 minutes. Limited parking (residential permit required in many areas), best for those who can walk or get dropped off.
  • Watchung Plaza: To NYC – 35-45 minutes. Small lot, fills quickly. Popular with South End residents.
  • Bay Street: To NYC – 35-40 minutes. Adjacent to downtown, walkable from nearby homes. No dedicated parking lot.
  • Montclair State University: To NYC – 30-40 minutes. Large parking garage, most reliable parking option. Slightly shorter commute due to express service.
  • Montclair Heights: To NYC – 35-45 minutes. Residential area with street parking challenges.
  • Mountain Avenue: To NYC – 40-50 minutes. Small lot in residential neighborhood.

Monthly Pass: Approximately $300-$350 depending on your destination

Train Schedule and Frequency

Weekday service runs approximately every 30-60 minutes during rush hour, with reduced frequency midday and on weekends. The trip involves a transfer at either Newark Broad Street or Secaucus Junction, depending on the train. Peak trains can be crowded, particularly the 7:00-8:30 AM departures.

Bus Service

  • NJ Transit Bus 28: Montclair to Port Authority via Bloomfield Avenue – 60-90 minutes depending on traffic
  • NJ Transit Bus 29: Alternate route to Port Authority
  • NJ Transit Bus 34: Additional coverage along Bloomfield Avenue corridor
  • DeCamp Bus 33: Commuter bus to Port Authority, popular with residents who prefer bus to train

Driving Commute

For those who drive to NYC:

  • To Midtown Manhattan: 16 miles, typically 35-60 minutes depending on traffic
  • To Downtown NYC: 20 miles, 45-75 minutes
  • Best Routes: Garden State Parkway to I-78 to Holland Tunnel, or Route 3 to Lincoln Tunnel
  • Parking in NYC: Monthly parking in Midtown runs $400-$600; many commuters use park-and-ride options

Highway Access

Montclair's location provides excellent highway connectivity:

  • Garden State Parkway: 10-15 minutes via Route 3 or I-280
  • I-280: Direct access from Montclair, connects to I-80 west and NYC east
  • Route 3: 5-10 minutes, connects to Lincoln Tunnel
  • I-78: 15 minutes via Garden State Parkway
  • I-80: 15-20 minutes via I-280

Local Transportation

Within Montclair, many residents find they can get by with one car or even car-free living:

  • Montclair trolley: Free downtown shuttle connecting train stations and shopping areas (weekends)
  • Bike-friendly: Growing network of bike lanes and paths
  • Walkability: High walk score in downtown areas for errands and dining

When helping buyers, I always ask about commute priorities. Being close to a specific train station can add $50,000-$100,000 to home values, so understanding your tolerance for walking or driving to the station helps us focus the search efficiently.

Lifestyle & Amenities in Montclair

Montclair isn't just a place to live – it's a lifestyle. The combination of arts, dining, outdoor recreation, and community events creates a richness that many residents say rivals living in Manhattan, but with a backyard and parking. Let me walk you through what makes life in Montclair special.

Arts & Culture

Montclair's arts scene rivals many major cities and is a primary draw for residents:

  • Montclair Art Museum (MAM): Renowned museum with exceptional collections of American art and Native American artifacts. The museum offers classes, workshops, and rotating exhibitions. Free admission for Montclair residents on certain days.
  • Wellmont Theater: Beautifully restored 1922 theater hosting major concerts, comedy shows, and events. Recent acts have included everything from rock legends to contemporary indie bands. The theater anchors downtown and brings energy to the area.
  • Montclair Film Festival: Annual festival founded by Stephen Colbert (Montclair resident) that attracts major talent and thousands of visitors. Screenings, Q&As with directors and actors, and year-round programming.
  • Live Theater: Multiple theater companies including Montclair State University's Peak Performances, the Studio Playhouse, and various community theaters. The high school theater program is renowned.
  • Art Galleries: Numerous galleries along Bloomfield Avenue and side streets showcasing local and regional artists. Monthly art walks and gallery openings.
  • Music Scene: Live music venues, jazz clubs, and the Montclair Orchestra providing classical programming.

Dining & Shopping

Montclair has become a culinary destination, with restaurants that would succeed in any major city:

  • Fine Dining: Laboratorio Kitchen (upscale Italian), Faubourg (French), and various chef-driven restaurants
  • Casual Favorites: Raymond's (brunch institution), Egan & Sons (Irish pub), Turtle + Wolf (American)
  • International Cuisine: Ethiopian, Thai, Indian, Mexican, Japanese, and more – reflecting Montclair's diversity
  • Coffee Culture: Multiple independent coffee shops including Red Eye Café, Java Love, and others
  • Shopping: Church Street and Bloomfield Avenue offer boutique clothing, home goods, books (watch for the new bookstore opening), and specialty foods
  • Grocery Options: Whole Foods, ShopRite, and various specialty markets including international grocers

Parks & Recreation

Despite its urban feel, Montclair offers abundant green space:

  • Brookdale Park: 121-acre Essex County park with sports fields, playgrounds, tennis courts, running track, and seasonal ice skating. Hosts concerts and events.
  • Eagle Rock Reservation: 400+ acres with hiking trails, NYC views, picnic areas, and the Essex County Environmental Center. The 9/11 memorial here is moving.
  • Edgemont Memorial Park: Beautiful neighborhood park with pond, playground, and memorial monuments. Popular for photography and quiet walks.
  • Mountainside Park: Sports fields, playground, and pool in the Mountainside section.
  • Presby Memorial Iris Gardens: Beautiful gardens with thousands of iris varieties, free and open to the public. Peak bloom in late May is spectacular.
  • YMCA: Full-service facility with pool, fitness center, and programs for all ages

Community Events & Organizations

Montclair has an active community calendar that helps newcomers quickly feel connected:

  • Montclair Farmers Market: Weekly market with local produce, prepared foods, and crafts (seasonal)
  • Jazz Festival: Annual outdoor music festival
  • Holiday Events: Tree lighting, holiday stroll, Fourth of July fireworks
  • 5K Races: Multiple charity runs throughout the year
  • Neighborhood Associations: Active groups throughout town organizing block parties, cleanups, and advocacy
  • Service Organizations: Rotary, Lions Club, and numerous volunteer opportunities
  • Religious Communities: Diverse houses of worship representing many faiths

Health & Wellness

Property Taxes & Cost of Living in Montclair

Let's talk honestly about taxes – they're an important factor in your homebuying decision. Montclair's property taxes are higher than the national average but competitive with similar Essex County suburbs. I always provide my clients with detailed tax estimates so there are no surprises.

Property Tax Overview

Montclair's property taxes fund the excellent schools, police and fire departments, road maintenance, and municipal services. The township is part of Essex County, which has higher taxes than some outer counties but also provides robust services and infrastructure.

Tax Appeals

If you believe your home is over-assessed, you have the right to appeal. The deadline is typically April 1st each year. I've helped many clients evaluate whether an appeal makes sense and can connect you with appraisers who specialize in tax appeals.

Other Cost of Living Considerations

  • Utilities: Expect $200-$400/month depending on home size and season (older homes may have higher heating costs)
  • Commuting Costs: NJ Transit monthly pass ~$300-$350, parking at some stations, or gas/tolls if driving
  • Home Insurance: Typically $1,500-$3,000/year depending on home value and coverage
  • Maintenance: Budget 1-2% of home value annually for maintenance on these older properties

Tax Deductions

Remember that property taxes and mortgage interest are generally tax-deductible (subject to current tax laws and limits). For many residents, the SALT deduction cap affects the tax benefit, so consult your tax advisor about your specific situation.

Comparison to Nearby Towns

Montclair's taxes are comparable to South Orange and Maplewood, slightly higher than Bloomfield, and lower than Millburn or Summit. The taxes reflect the quality of schools and services – many residents view the higher taxes as an investment in their children's education and community amenities.
