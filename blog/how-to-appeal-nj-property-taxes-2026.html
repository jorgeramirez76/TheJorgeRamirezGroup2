You appeal the assessment, not the rate

This is the part most homeowners get wrong. You cannot appeal your town's tax rate — that's set by the municipal, county, and school budgets. What you can challenge is your home's assessed value. If the town is taxing you as though your home is worth more than it actually is, an appeal corrects that — and because the rate is then applied to a lower value, your bill drops.

And it keeps dropping. A successful appeal generally holds until your town's next revaluation, so a one-time effort can save you money for several years running. That's what makes it one of the highest-return hours a NJ homeowner can spend.

Step 1: Are you actually over-assessed?

Don't appeal on a hunch. Run the math first. Divide your assessed value by your county's equalization ratio (also called the average ratio, published yearly) to get the market value the town is effectively taxing you on. Compare that to what your home would realistically sell for today.

The margin matters: New Jersey allows a tolerance band — the Chapter 123 "common level range." Your assessment has to exceed that margin, not just be a little high, for an appeal to succeed. If your implied value is only slightly above market, you likely don't have a winning case.

Step 2: Build your evidence

Appeals are won on comparable sales. Pull three to five recent, arm's-length sales of similar homes in your town — same style, size, and neighborhood — that support a lower value than your assessment implies. A recent appraisal strengthens higher-value cases. Photos of genuine condition issues (deferred maintenance, functional problems) can also support a lower value.

What doesn't help: asking prices, hearsay, distressed or non-arm's-length sales, and "my neighbor pays less." Bring real, recent, comparable transactions.

Step 3: File, present, and decide

  1. File on time. Submit your appeal to the County Board of Taxation by the deadline — generally April 1, or May 1 in revaluation/reassessment towns. Confirm the current year's date; deadlines vary.
  2. Pay the modest fee. Filing fees scale with assessed value and are typically small for a normal home.
  3. Present your case. At the hearing, walk through your comparable sales and any condition issues. Keep it factual and organized.
  4. Get the decision. If the board agrees you're over-assessed beyond the allowed margin, it lowers your assessment — and your future bills drop.

In the highest-tax state in the country, an over-assessment is the most expensive mistake you can make on autopilot. Fifteen minutes of math tells you whether it's worth fighting.

Know the risk

An appeal isn't free of downside. The board can affirm your assessment, or — rarely — find your home is under-assessed, in which case the value stands or could rise. That's exactly why you appeal only when your comps clearly support a lower value. Straightforward residential appeals are often handled by the homeowner; complex or high-value cases can warrant an attorney who specializes in NJ tax appeals.

Frequently asked questions

Can you really lower your property taxes in NJ?

You can't lower the rate, but you can lower your assessed value if you're over-assessed — and that cuts your bill, often for years until the next revaluation. The key is recent comparable sales showing the assessment is too high.

What is the deadline to appeal NJ property taxes?

Generally April 1 with the County Board of Taxation, or May 1 in towns that did a revaluation or reassessment. Deadlines vary by county and year — confirm the current date before relying on it.

How do I know if my home is over-assessed?

Divide your assessed value by your county's equalization ratio to get the market value you're taxed on. If that's meaningfully above today's sale value — beyond NJ's Chapter 123 margin — you may have a case.

What evidence do I need?

Three to five recent, arm's-length sales of comparable homes in your town. A recent appraisal helps in higher-value cases, and photos of condition issues can support a lower value. Asking prices and distressed sales carry little weight.

How much does it cost, and is it risky?

Filing fees are modest and scale with assessed value (often $5–$150). The risk is the board affirming the assessment, or rarely finding it under-assessed. Appeal only when comps clearly support a lower value; complex cases may warrant an attorney.

How much can a successful appeal save me?

Cutting an assessment by $50,000 in a ~2.2% town saves roughly $1,100/year, recurring until the next revaluation — often thousands over several years. Because it compounds annually, even a moderate reduction is usually worth it.
