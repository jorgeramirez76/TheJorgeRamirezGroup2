NJ has the highest property taxes in America — here's the real number

New Jersey's average effective property tax rate is roughly 2.23%, the highest of any U.S. state. On a $700,000 home, that's about $15,600 a year — more than $1,300 a month before you've paid a dollar of mortgage. For a New Yorker used to property taxes baked invisibly into rent or a condo's common charges, it's the single biggest sticker shock of the move.

But the statewide average hides the real opportunity. Property taxes in NJ are local. Two commuter towns 15 minutes apart, with homes at the same price, can hand you bills that differ by thousands of dollars a year. Knowing how to read that difference is one of the highest-return things a buyer can do.

Effective rate vs. headline rate — the only comparison that matters

The effective rate is your annual tax bill divided by your home's actual market value. It's the only apples-to-apples way to compare towns, because NJ municipalities assess homes at different fractions of market value. A town can advertise a low nominal rate while assessing close to full value — and end up more expensive than a town with a scary-looking nominal rate that assesses low.

Skip the confusion: compare the actual annual dollar bill on a home at your price point, town by town. That number already bakes in everything.

Important: Effective tax rates are certified each year and shift with budgets and revaluations. The figures below are directional, to show how wide the gap gets — always confirm the current year's certified rate with the town tax assessor or your county board of taxation before relying on it.

How to use property taxes to your advantage

  1. Compare the dollar bill, not the rate. Ask for the actual annual taxes on every home you tour and on a "typical" home in any town you're considering.
  2. Fold taxes into your monthly math up front. In high-tax towns, taxes alone can add $1,000–$2,000+ a month — that changes your real budget and your pre-approval.
  3. Weigh schools and taxes together. Sometimes a lower bill reflects a leaner municipal budget, not a weaker district. Evaluate both.
  4. Check for a recent or upcoming revaluation. A town-wide revaluation can move your assessment — and your bill — meaningfully. Ask before you buy.
  5. Know your appeal rights. If you believe a home is over-assessed, NJ has an annual tax-appeal window. It won't change the town's rate, but it can correct an inflated assessment.

Frequently asked questions

Why are New Jersey property taxes so high?

NJ has the highest effective property tax rate in the U.S. (~2.23%) largely because the state funds schools, municipal services, and county government mainly through local property taxes. Top-performing school districts drive higher local levies — which is why many of the best-school commuter towns also carry the biggest bills.

What is the effective property tax rate, and why does it matter more?

It's your annual bill divided by your home's actual market value. It matters more than a town's published rate because NJ towns assess at different fractions of value, so nominal rates aren't comparable. Compare effective rates — or simply the actual annual dollar bill on a home at your price point.

How much can property taxes differ between NJ commuter towns?

In the same county and price range, the annual bill can differ by $3,000+ a year. Over a typical seven-year ownership, that's $21,000+ — money buyers routinely overlook by focusing only on price and rate.

Which NJ commuter towns have the lowest property taxes?

In the NYC commuter belt, Clark, Fanwood, and Scotch Plains in Union County have historically carried lower effective rates than several premium neighbors at comparable prices, with NJ Transit access. Rates are certified yearly and shift — confirm the current figure with the town assessor or county board of taxation.

Do lower property taxes mean worse schools?

Not necessarily. The highest-tax towns often have top districts, but several lower-rate commuter towns still have strong schools. Evaluate schools and effective rate together — a lower bill can simply reflect a more efficient budget.

How do I factor property taxes into my real monthly cost?

Add the annual tax bill to mortgage principal and interest, insurance, and any NJ Transit pass, then divide by 12. In high-tax towns, taxes alone can add $1,000–$2,000+ a month and change how much house you qualify for. Use each town's effective rate, not the county average.
