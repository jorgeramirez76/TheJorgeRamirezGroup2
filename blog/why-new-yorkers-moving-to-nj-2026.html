The exodus, by the numbers

New Jersey home prices rose nearly 6% in early 2026 while the broader U.S. market grew about 0.5% — a roughly tenfold gap that points straight at demand, not a national trend. The state's median single-family price reached roughly $563,000–$570,000 by spring 2026, up about 3.3% year over year, even as the frantic 2021–2022 pace cooled and inventory slowly improved.

That gap is the story. Money leaving New York City and Hoboken is concentrating in a narrow band of commuter towns, and there simply aren't enough homes near train stations to absorb it. The result is the bidding-war market transplants keep reading about: well-priced homes in premium commuter towns routinely going under contract within 7–21 days, frequently over asking.

Why New Yorkers are leaving the city for NJ

It's rarely one reason. It's the stack. Here's what I hear at the kitchen table from nearly every NYC family I work with:

  • Price per square foot. The single biggest driver. A budget that buys a one-bedroom in Manhattan buys a full single-family home with a yard 35 minutes away on the train.
  • Space and a yard. Remote and hybrid work made a home office and outdoor space non-negotiable for a lot of households that tolerated 700 square feet pre-2020.
  • Schools. NJ's top public districts rival private schools families were paying $50K+ a year for in the city. That tuition saving alone can cover a meaningful chunk of a mortgage.
  • The NYC tax math. NYC residents pay a city income tax on top of state tax. A NJ resident commuting to Manhattan legally avoids the NYC resident city tax — a real, recurring saving that offsets NJ's higher property taxes for many earners.
  • The one-seat train. Midtown Direct drops you at Penn Station with no transfer. For a lot of buyers, a 35-minute train ride beats a 35-minute subway-plus-walk commute they already had inside the city.

How to actually win a home in this market

In a bidding-war market, you win on terms — not just the number. Transplants coming from the city are often surprised that the highest bid doesn't always take the house. Sellers want certainty. Here's what consistently wins in NJ commuter towns in 2026:

  1. Get fully underwritten, not just pre-approved. An underwritten approval lets you compete with cash-like confidence and shorten your mortgage contingency.
  2. Be surgical with contingencies. You don't have to waive everything — but knowing which contingencies a specific seller actually cares about is the difference between winning and overpaying. That's local knowledge, not a formula.
  3. Flex on the seller's timeline. Offering the closing and possession dates the seller wants is often worth more to them than another $10K.
  4. Move fast. When homes go in 7–14 days, "we'll see it next weekend" means you've already lost it. Be ready to tour day one and decide same day.
  5. Work with someone who knows the listing agents. I've spent years building relationships with the agents on the other side of these deals — and as someone who has personally bought, renovated, and sold homes as an investor, I read condition and value the way the listing side does. That's the edge that wins the close calls.

The buyers who lose in this market are the ones treating a $800K NJ purchase like a casual weekend browse. The ones who win treat it like the competitive process it actually is — with a local guide who's done it from both sides.

Frequently asked questions

Why are so many New Yorkers moving to New Jersey in 2026?

Mainly space and price per square foot. A budget that buys a one-bedroom in Manhattan or Hoboken buys a three- or four-bedroom house with a yard in towns like Maplewood, Cranford, or Bloomfield — plus stronger public schools, a one-seat Midtown Direct train, and no NYC resident city income tax. That stack pushed NJ prices up nearly 6% in early 2026 versus about 0.5% nationally.

Is it cheaper to live in New Jersey than New York City?

For most families, yes — on housing and taxes combined. NJ has the highest property tax rate in the country (~2.23%), but NYC residents pay a city income tax on top of state tax that NJ commuters legally avoid. Lower price per square foot, no city income tax, and strong public schools usually put commuting NJ households ahead overall.

Which NJ towns are New Yorkers buying in most?

The Midtown Direct line — Maplewood, South Orange, Montclair, Summit, Millburn/Short Hills, Chatham, Madison — plus Hoboken/Jersey City stay-close moves and value plays like Cranford, Westfield, Bloomfield, Rahway, and Metuchen. The common thread is a direct or near-direct NJ Transit ride to Penn Station.

How fast is the train from NJ to NYC?

On Midtown Direct in 2026: Maplewood ~30 min, Millburn 35, Short Hills 37, Summit 44, Chatham 52, Madison 55. On the Northeast Corridor: Newark Penn ~18, Elizabeth 25, Metropark 38. Many are one-seat rides with no transfer.

Are NJ home prices going to keep rising in 2026?

Most forecasts expect 3–5% annual gains through 2026 with no crash, supported by strong employment, tight inventory, and continued inflow from NYC. North Jersey commuter counties are appreciating fastest because demand outruns the supply of homes near stations.

How do I win a house in a NJ bidding war?

Win on terms, not just price: get fully underwritten before you tour, be surgical (not reckless) with contingencies, flex on the seller's closing/possession dates, and move fast — well-priced homes go in 7–21 days. A local agent who knows the listing agents and reads condition like an investor is the edge in close calls.
