How does the train line affect home value and commute quality?

In North and Central Jersey, the line your town sits on matters as much as the distance to Manhattan. Towns on the Morris & Essex Line with Midtown Direct service — places like Summit, Chatham, Madison, Millburn, Maplewood, and South Orange — offer a one-seat ride into New York Penn Station. Raritan Valley Line towns such as Cranford, Westfield, Fanwood, and Garwood typically require a transfer at Newark Penn Station during most of the day, which adds time and a little friction to every trip.

Buyers pay for that difference. One-seat-ride towns typically command a price premium over comparable towns a transfer away, and homes within walking distance of the station tend to hold value especially well. The flip side: Raritan Valley towns often give you noticeably more house for the money, and for many commuters the transfer adds only ten to fifteen minutes.

What counts as a good door-to-door commute?

Measure the whole trip, not the scheduled train time. Door-to-door includes the walk or drive to the station, parking (some NJ towns have multi-year waiting lists for resident permits), the train ride, any transfer, and the subway or walk on the Manhattan side. As a rule of thumb, under an hour door-to-door feels easy long-term, 60 to 75 minutes is the range most NJ commuters actually live with, and past 90 minutes the commute starts to shape your whole week.

How should you use a commute score when shortlisting towns?

Treat the score as a filter, not a verdict. Use it to narrow the map to three or four towns that fit your commute ceiling and budget, then compare those finalists on schools, taxes, downtown feel, and housing stock. A town that scores slightly lower on commute but wins on everything else is often the right call.

Should you test the commute before you buy?

Yes — do the actual ride at rush hour before you make an offer. Park where you would park, catch the train you would catch, and time the full trip to your office door. Schedules, express patterns, and crowding vary by line and by hour, and a 7:12 a.m. express is a different experience than a midday local. One test ride tells you more than any spreadsheet.
