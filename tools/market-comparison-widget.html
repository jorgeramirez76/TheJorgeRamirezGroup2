Should you compare towns by median price or price per square foot?

Both — because each one lies a little on its own. A town's median sale price is skewed by what happened to sell that quarter: a run of large colonials pushes the median up even if nothing about the market changed. Price per square foot corrects for home size, so it's the better yardstick when two towns have very different housing stock — say, Cape Cods in one and center-hall colonials in the other. But price per square foot ignores lot size, condition, and renovation level, so use it to frame the comparison, then look at actual sold listings to see what the numbers are hiding.

What does days on market actually tell you?

Days on market is a speed gauge. When well-priced homes in a town typically go under contract in a week or two, sellers have the leverage and buyers need to come prepared. When listings sit for a month or more, there's room to negotiate. Compare the figure by price band, not just by town — a starter home and a two-million-dollar listing in the same ZIP code often live in completely different markets.

Why compare property-tax rates instead of tax bills?

New Jersey tax bills reflect both the local rate and the home's assessed value, so a big bill sometimes just means a big house. The effective tax rate — what you pay as a percentage of market value — is what lets you compare towns fairly. Two NJ towns can have similar average bills but meaningfully different rates, and at any given purchase price the lower-rate town leaves more room in your monthly payment. School quality works the same way: strong districts are typically capitalized into home prices, so you're paying for them either way — the question is whether you'll use them.

Do the seasons change what the numbers say?

Absolutely. The NJ spring market typically brings the most inventory and the most competition; late fall and winter run slower, with fewer listings and softer stats. That means comparing this month's numbers to last month's mostly measures the calendar. Compare year over year instead, and if you're weighing two towns, pull the same period for both.
